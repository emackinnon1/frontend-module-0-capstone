I think the one hobby that I have stuck with the longest and been the most consistently passionate about is health and fitness. I love it. I would play sports and be active all day if I could. I love setting fitness goals and breaking them, and seeing how far I can push myself mentally and physically.
My current exercise fixation is calisthenics, otherwise known as bodyweight fitness or street workouts. It is has a lot of great benefits, like the fact that you can do it anywhere! You don't need a gym because there is minimal equipment involved. It involves a lot of crossover ideas and techniques from gymnastics with less of an emphasis on aesthetics and more of an emphasis on strength building and hypertrophy. It involves a lot of compound exercises that recruit multiple muscle groups, resulting in more calories burned. There are cons as well, like it being harder to measure progress (Precisely how much harder is a one arm push up than a regular pushup?), but overall it is a really great fitness journey to embark on.

Where it all started

I used to run 5 or more miles every day (mostly from the cops, obviously). (insert running from cop pic)
On my off days I played soccer, my other love. They were my drugs, my stress relievers and a permanent (or so I thought) part of my lifestyle.
Just before I turned 27, disaster hit. I had my 4th knee surgery. Afterwards, the doctor told me I couldn't run anymore and I would need a total knee replacement in 10 years at the latest (if I was lucky) but most likely a bit earlier. I had no cartilage left and grade 4 (it only goes up to grade 5) arthritis. I was absolutely devastated. It's amazing how much of your personal identity and wellbeing is derived from physical activity. Something you take for granted about your body can just be gone one day. It was eye-opening.
(insert crying pic)

A few weeks after my surgery, I switched to cycling for cardio and bought a bike. A few years after I started getting serious about running, I started weight training, but did it purely supplementally and didn't really set many goals besides "putting on muscle mass" and "getting stronger". So I continued my weight training with the same vague goals, but I was bored. Also during this time, I moved to Germany where, unbeknownst to me at the time, calisthenics is much more popular. I found all these outdoor workout spots around the Munich and saw people doing pullups, pushups and dips, etc. I liked the idea of exercising outside, so I began to frequent these outdoor gyms.
And then, one day, I saw this one guy doing handstand pushups. *record scratch* I did a double take. I stopped and creepily stared. I had never seen that before. "Woah. I'm learning that. I don't care how long it takes me," I thought to myself. Commence the endless Google searches and hours spend watching YouTube videos on handstands and workout programs to achieve them.
I put a rudimentary routine together and starting working on it every other day. As I did all this research, I found other exercises and things that I wanted to learn. Calisthenics is full of awesome-looking exercises and skills to work towards! After about 3 months of hard work, I could do this:

It was sloppy, but it was my first real progress!! I was thrilled. More importantly, I was hooked. I found my new drug.
Since then, I have learned a lot more. Through hours of research and many trials and errors, I have learned the ins and outs of building a program specifically tailored for bodyweight fitness, learned how to prevent and recover from injuries, how to be systematic in my approach to it (you can't just go up in dumbbell weight to make the exercise harder, you have to change body your position), and a lot more.
